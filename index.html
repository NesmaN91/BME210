Course Requirements Overview

Welcome to our course! Below is a clear guide to each course requirement, what it involves, why it matters, how to do it well, when it’s due, and how it’s graded.

What it is & Why it matters

Every week you will complete the assigned readings and create an “interconnected map” reflection. You’ll draw a simple concept map or outline showing how key ideas from the readings connect, and write a short reflection about those connections. This turns passive reading into active learning and helps you deepen understanding, retention, and critical thinking.

How to do it well
  1. Do the weekly reading actively: take notes/highlight key points and questions.
  2. Create your concept map: map key themes and show how ideas connect (boxes/arrows, flowchart, or outline).
  3. Write a short reflection (150-250 words): explain the connections, insights, and why it matters; use specific examples or a quote.
  4. Submit on time each week: follow the schedule (typically due by the end of the week).
When it’s due

Due weekly (12 weeks total). Check the course schedule for the exact weekly due day/time.

Grading

About 5 points each week (5 × 12 = 60). Full credit requires both a map and reflection with genuine engagement, connections across ideas, and specific support from the text.

What it is & Why it matters

Throughout the semester, you will meet with your Professional Learning Community (PLC) to discuss the questions that emerge after completing the assigned readings for each cycle. Your PLC will collect the thoughts, ideas, and reactions shared in the group discussion in a memo. The memo should contain the names of members who were present and contributed substantially. If someone’s name is not included, it will be assumed they did not meet their obligation and will not receive the points for that memo. Responses to each question should be between 250-300 words.

What you’ll do
  • Participate in small-group meetings (in class or as instructed).
  • Submit short PLC memos after certain meetings, summarizing what your group discussed and what you learned.
How to do it well
  • Be prepared: complete readings before PLC time so you can contribute.
  • Keep notes: jot key points during meetings for easier memo writing.
  • Write clear memos: focus, key conclusions, your takeaway, and any next steps.
  • Be consistent: show up and contribute respectfully and constructively.
When it’s due

PLC meetings and memos occur periodically throughout the course. Follow the schedule for specific weeks/due dates.

Grading

Graded on participation quality and memo completeness/insight. Strong work shows you contributed, captured key ideas, and reflected thoughtfully.

What it is & Why it matters

Attendance is worth 60 points across 29 sessions (about 2 points per class)+ 20 points for your class notebook. Being present supports your learning through discussion, activities, and community building.

How it works
  • Each session you attend and participate in contributes points toward the total.
  • If you must miss due to an unavoidable issue, communicate as soon as possible.
  • Be punctual and stay the full session.
Tips to maximize points
  • Prioritize class time like an appointment.
  • Communicate early if an emergency occurs.
  • Be mentally present and engaged.
Grading

60/60 for full attendance+20 points for your notebook. Missing classes reduces points (roughly 2 points per absence). Policies for excused absences will be clarified in course policies.

What it is & Why it matters

This course emphasizes discussion. Participation points reflect your engagement in whole-class and small-group discussions across the semester.

How to earn points
  • Come prepared: complete readings and bring at least one question or idea.
  • Contribute thoughtfully: ask questions, build on peers’ ideas, and connect to course concepts.
  • Listen and engage: participation also includes respectful listening and responding.
  • Be consistent: aim to contribute regularly throughout the course.
Grading

Based on frequency, quality/relevance, preparedness, and respectful engagement. Strong participation adds value to shared learning.

What it is & Why it matters

A one-time reflection exploring your identity and how it shapes your perspectives. Self-awareness is foundational for intercultural learning.

How to do it well
  • Be personal and sincere (write in first person).
  • Include examples and experiences that shaped you.
  • Connect to course themes when possible.

When it’s due

Typically early in the course (check the schedule for the exact date).

Grading

Graded on completion and depth. Full credit shows genuine reflection, clarity, and attention to the prompt.

What it is & Why it matters

Interview someone about their life experiences and write a short report connecting their story to course themes. This builds empathy, listening skills, and qualitative thinking.

What to do
  1. Select a person willing to share.
  2. Prepare guiding questions.
  3. Conduct the interview (take notes or record with permission).
  4. Write the report: who you interviewed, key life highlights, and reflection/connection to course themes.
How to excel
  • Use specific details/quotes to show strong listening.
  • Connect clearly to course concepts.
  • Include your takeaway and what you learned.
  • Write clearly and keep a professional tone.
When it’s due

Typically mid-to-late course (check the schedule for the exact date).

Grading

Graded on completion, clarity of the interview-based narrative, and thoughtful reflection/connection to course themes.

Major Assignments

This course is grounded in identity and culture and this assignment is an opportunity for you to engage with your own. In this paper, you will expand on the identity breakdown activity completed in class by describing the different constructs you’ve documented and how they contribute to your identity. This paper should have at least 2 pages (double-spaced, 12-point font) of text, but it can be longer if you include other media sources (pictures, drawings, poems, etc.). Include Theme, Insights, Connections, and Reflection.

To encourage you to grow your personal/professional network and experience learning about another language/culture, you will interview an adult from a different background and document their life history. Sample questions will be provided, but you are encouraged to develop some of your own. The main foci of this interview should be: 1) To describe their background. 2) To describe their education history. 3) A comparison between yours and their culture. This report should be at least 2 pages (double-spaced, 12-point font).

This semester-long project asks you to explore culture, identity, language, power, and lived experience through research, reflection, and interaction. Instead of a traditional final exam, you will develop this project in stages across the semester, ending with a final paper and an in-class presentation.You may focus on your own community, a community you belong to, or a culture you want to understand more deeply for personal or professional reasons.

Intercultural Competence Project (100 pts)

A semester-long signature project exploring culture, language, and power.

Dimension 1: Knowledge

Identify a culture/community; baseline of beliefs and positionality (2–3 pages).

Dimension 2: Education

Research historical and linguistic contexts via academic sources (2–3 pages, APA).

Dimension 3: Skills

Community interaction/interview and reflective commentary on experience.

Dimension 4: Relate

Synthesis of how your understanding changed (1–2 pages).

Final Phase

Final paper + showcase components are included in the 100-point IC project total-IC Four Dimensions 40 Points-Showcase Presentation 20 points-Final IC Research Paper 40 points.

AI Usage

If you use artificial intelligence (AI) to help, write your paper and it plagiarizes material or makes other mistakes, you are responsible for this. Plagiarized work will receive an F (zero points). You could use it but be careful and apply the human in the loop approach, so it is accurate and doesn’t violate the integrity part or automation plagiarism. If you are to use AI for minor tasks, be critical and don’t copy past, use it in an interactive way to help you with what you are struggling with and I recommend using this model to avoid misuse and ethical issues. For more details on how to keep academic integrity, check resources I added to Canvas.

Academic Integrity

NAU expects honesty, trustworthiness, and responsibility. Plagiarism includes using 3+ words in succession without citation. Review full policies at policy.nau.edu.

Learning Outcomes (SLO)

  1. Analyze critical issues of multicultural education in sociopolitical contexts (TESOL 2a, 2b, 2e).
  2. Synthesize principles to plan for culturally relevant environments (TESOL 3a).
  3. Describe influence of social/political identities (race, class, gender) on school policies.
  4. Associate cross-cultural experiences by conducting interviews (TESOL 2d).
  5. Analyze sociocultural narratives of diverse student backgrounds (TESOL 2c).
  6. Review current research via written discussions.
  7. Apply knowledge of political variables to English learning (TESOL 1b).
  8. Understand learner variables in English acquisition.
  9. Apply cultural values to ESL teaching.
  10. Address effects of racism and stereotyping in ESL.
  11. Use resources to learn student cultures.
  12. Explore schooling success factors (religion, orientation, class).
  13. Apply knowledge of U.S. immigration patterns.
  14. Review case studies and student narratives.
  15. Explore school climate and structural issues (safety, class size).
  16. Explore inequities and productive resolutions.
  17. Examine intergroup relations and theories of conflict.
  18. Analyze identities that perpetuate inequality in school structures.
